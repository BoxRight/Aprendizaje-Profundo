Maximum Velocity of Human Movement used for
Human Fall Detection using a Support Vector
Machine classifier for Doppler Radar Sensors Data
Anonymous

Abstract— The feasibility of detecting falls among different
human activities based on micro-Doppler signatures is
investigated. This study uses the University of Glasgow Radar
Signature dataset, consisting of recordings from 72 persons
performing six different activities, including falls. The maximum
velocity that each person reached while performing an activity was
extracted from the Doppler spectrogram and used to train a
Support Vector, to distinguish falls from other activities. The
model’s performance is evaluated, to assess the potential of using
maximum speed as the classification feature on real-world
applications for fall detection.
Keywords—Fall detection, Support Vector Machines.

II. RELATED WORK
Nooruddin et al [5] reviewed many FDS, spanning a very
comprehensive spectrum of technologies, including singlesensor and multi-sensor systems, and wearable and external
systems. They evaluated the systems using the Accuracy,
Sensitivity and Specificity metrics typically used in fall
detection literature to rate these systems. They concluded that
the number of sensors does not normally impact the system`s
accuracy. They also noted that accuracy is more dependent on
the features selected for classification in the system. Their article
identifies strengths and weaknesses for the different sensor
technologies: accelerometers are the best for wearable solutions;
Kinect sensors cannot be used outdoors because sunlight affects
their accuracy and their ability to estimate depth. Sound sensors
are ubiquitous and nonintrusive but can generate false alarms
due to ambient noise and raise privacy concerns. Wearable
motion sensors are compact and respect privacy, but they require
frequent charging. Computer-vision or surveillance-based fall
detection systems need huge amounts of processing power. Hsu
et al [8] state that all those issues can be avoided with millimeterwave radar or microwave sensors, using electromagnetic waves
Doppler effect. Hu, Cao et all explore the details of Wi-fi and
radar sensors [9]: they are noninvasive, they ensure privacy.
Radio wave technology can detect through obstructions like
walls, is not affected by environmental conditions such as poor
lighting, and can operate efficiently in low power. Another
important factor for FDS, is cost, since for FDS be practical, they
should be designed to be both very reliable and affordable, so
they are widely available for people who need them.
In the past few years, deep learning and related classification
technologies have gained considerable interest in radar-based
FDS, because they extract salient features from radar signals in
an automatic fashion. The problem is that deep learning methods
require a large amount of training data, and the actual fall
detection requires a large amount of computational and memory
resources. Most current deep learning approaches require a long
latency even at the inference/testing stage, because of complex
data processing methods or deep neural networks. Current
research is very focused on accuracy, ignoring computational
cost. Li, Le Kernec et al [10] have explored the requirements of
FDS systems to be deployed on resource-constrained platforms.
The consideration of the balance between efficiency and
performance is a main item when selecting the most suitable
algorithms. A natural step, after classification algorithms
development is how to approach a real-time implementation of
radar-based fall detection in constrained platforms.

III. METHOD
A. Software tools
MATLAB R2025b is used to process the data and to test the
different data processing methods to transform the input signal,
extract the relevant features and identify falls. The steps of the
method to train the model and to classify the data are
documented as MATLAB code. Different MATLAB libraries
were used to process and transform radar data, for training the
model and for testing it. The MATLAB code is useful for rapid
developing and testing different models and approaches, and it

is also useful to write production quality software programs for
the training component and for the classifier component.
B. Training and test data
The University of Glasgow Radar Signature dataset [11] was
used. The data gathering process is documented at the URL
shown in reference [11]. In summary, data was generated by a
Frequency Modulated Continuous Wave (FMCW) radar,
operating at the 5.8 GHz band, with a chirp (a frequency sweep)
that was repeated each millisecond and to digitize it, was
sampled 128 times per sweep. Note that the term “beat” is
sometimes used instead of “sweep” in FDS articles. The signal
has a 400 MHz bandwidth. The data contains a record for each
sample, that is a complex number, where the In-phase data for
the sample is the real part and the Quadrature data for the sample
is the complex part. Each file contains either 10 seconds or 5
seconds of radar data for a motion capture, i.e. 1,280,000 records
or 640,000 records. Also, each file includes four records; the
first one contains the band (5.8 GHz), the second has the chirp
duration (1 ms), the third has the number of samples per sweep
(128) and the fourth shows the bandwidth (400 MHz).
There is a total of 1,754 files, recorded by 72 participants
aged between 21 and 98 years old. The total space required by
the data files is 15.9 Gigabytes. Each participant performed six
activities in front of the radar device. The radar has one antenna
for sending the signals and one for receiving the signals. The
person does not use any sensors. Older individuals didn’t
perform the “Falling” activity, to avoid the possibility of injuries
for them. Because of this, it can be seen on Table I that there are
less files for activity number 6: Falling. Each activity was
repeated 3 times. The activities were performed indoors. Only a
single person is measured each time. The following table

D. Feature Selection and Extraction.
Data files contain a record for each one of the 128 samples
taken for each “chirp” echoed by the target, i.e. the person who
is performing the activity in front of the radar. A chirp is sent
every millisecond, and the activity is recorded for 5 seconds,
except for the “Walking back and forth” activity which lasts 10
seconds: 5 for walking forth and 5 for walking back. The first
step is to rearrange the data in a matrix where we have one row
for each millisecond, and each row has 128 columns, with the
samples. The next step is to take the Fast Fourier Transform
(FFT) for the matrix, using a Hamming window to reduce the
noise. This transforms the matrix to Velocity vs Time. If we
apply a second FFT, we obtain the Doppler matrix, the
spectrogram, which characterizes each action. Let’s look at the
spectrograms for Persons 36, 37 and 38, during two activities:

V. DISCUSSION
The metrics obtained for this SVM are not good enough to
be used for a real application. There may be two reasons: either
the feature is not adequate, or the model is not adequate. To
eliminate the possibility that the problem lies on SVM, further
work is needed: 1) Establish a baseline, using Logistic
Regression. 2) Train two other models, k-NN and Random
Forest. The comparison of the results of the SVM against these
other models should allow to evaluate if the problem lies with
the usage of SVM. To eliminate the possibility that the problem
lies on the selected feature, a pair of new features should be used:
Standard Deviation of the Velocity and Acceleration look
promising. The state of the art of fall detection obtains higher
metrics than what was achieved on this work, so this further
work is desirable. Using a set of more sophisticated features than
those used on this study may also be done. For example, in [20]
combined Range and Doppler features and obtained very high
metrics.
VI. CONCLUSIONS
A better model should be built, trying different classifiers
and different features. A better model should be useful to
establish a baseline for developing a system that can be used in
real world applications, and this is desirable, because the costs
of current radar-based configurations are prohibitive for massive
deployments at hospitals and assisted care facilities, and for
domestic use.
Li, Le Kernec et al [9] reported in 2023 a very exciting
technique where they used a magnitude thresholding approach,
which allows to pinpoint the region where the activities features
are located, separating them from the signal’s noise. Also, this
technique reduces the amount of data to be processed, reducing
both the training time and memory footprint.
VII. LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
This model was not designed to be able to run on a
continuous basis: it assumed that data is provided in files that
record 5-second intervals. A workable solution should be able to
process data on a continuous basis. A promising project is to use
Li and Le Kernec’s techniques [9] and determine how the model
should be built such as to be able to process continuous signals.
The use of boosting algorithms like XGBoost or LightGBM may
help to solve the problem of processing data on a continuous
basis.
